Also called **retrieval practice** or the **testing effect**. The basic finding: being tested on material produces better long-term retention than studying the same material for the same amount of time.

This is counterintuitive. Taking a test *feels* like assessment, not learning. But the act of retrieval itself is what strengthens memory.

## The Roediger & Karpicke (2006) Study

Students studied a prose passage then either:
- **Restudied** the passage four times (SSSS)
- **Studied** once then **tested** three times (STTT)

One week later: the STTT group retained **50% more**.

> [!quote] Roediger & Karpicke, 2006
> "Repeated retrieval practice produced dramatic benefits to long-term retention relative to repeated studying."

## Why It Works

1. **Retrieval strengthens the memory trace** — the effort of searching for something in long-term memory and finding it makes the pathway more accessible next time
2. **Testing reveals gaps** — you discover what you don't actually know (vs. what feels familiar when you reread it)
3. **Elaboration during retrieval** — pulling up a memory often triggers connections to related knowledge

> [!note] Familiarity vs. recall
> When you reread notes, everything feels familiar. This creates **fluency illusion** — a false sense of knowing. Active recall bypasses this entirely. If you can't retrieve it, you don't know it.

## How to Practice Active Recall

**Flashcards** — the classic. Works well for facts, vocabulary, formulas. Pair with [[Spaced Repetition]] for maximum effect.

**The blank page method** — after reading a chapter/watching a lecture, close everything and write down everything you remember. Then check what you missed.

**Self-quizzing** — turn headings into questions before reading, then try to answer them after.

**Teaching** — the [[The Feynman Technique]] is essentially active recall forced through the medium of explanation.

**Practice tests** — do old exams under realistic conditions. More effective than re-reading the textbook.

> [!tip] Low-stakes testing is still testing
> You don't need formal exams. Asking yourself "what were the main points of that chapter?" while walking to the kitchen counts. The habit matters more than the format.

## Common Objections

*"But I don't remember enough to even start"* — that's fine. Struggling and failing to recall, then checking the answer, still produces better retention than passive review. The failure is part of the process.

*"It takes longer"* — it does, per session. But you need far fewer sessions. Total time to durable memory is lower.
