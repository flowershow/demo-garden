Interleaving is the practice of mixing different subjects or problem types within a single study session, rather than finishing one topic completely before moving to the next (called **blocked practice**).

It feels worse. It is better.

## Blocked vs Interleaved

**Blocked practice:**
> Monday: 30 addition problems → 30 subtraction problems → 30 multiplication problems

**Interleaved practice:**
> Monday: addition, multiplication, subtraction, addition, subtraction, multiplication...

Students who practice in a blocked format consistently *feel* they're learning more and perform better on immediate tests. On delayed tests — the ones that measure actual long-term learning — interleaved students win, often by large margins.

> [!note] The difficulty is the signal
> Interleaving is harder because you have to **identify** what kind of problem you're facing before applying a strategy. Blocked practice removes that step. But in real life (and on real exams), you always have to identify the problem type first. Interleaving trains that skill.

## Why It Works

Two mechanisms:

1. **Discrimination learning** — switching between topics forces you to notice what makes each different, building clearer category boundaries
2. **Retrieval practice** — after studying topic B, returning to topic A forces you to re-retrieve it from memory (see [[Active Recall]])

Both mechanisms are related to [[How We Learn]] — specifically the principle that *effortful* encoding is more durable.

## Practical Application

- **Multiple subjects in one session**: instead of "chemistry day" and "history day", mix them
- **Problem set interleaving**: if you have 20 calculus problems of 4 types, mix them rather than doing 5 of each in sequence
- **Mixed flashcard decks** in [[Spaced Repetition]] tools — don't study one deck to completion before another

> [!tip] How much to interleave?
> This isn't a reason to never focus. Early in learning a new concept, some blocked practice makes sense — you need enough exposure to build a basic schema. Interleaving pays off more once you have something to interleave *with*.

> [!warning] Don't confuse interleaving with randomness
> The topics being interleaved should be **related** — same domain, same course. Mixing calculus with German vocabulary doesn't produce the discrimination benefit.

## Personal Note

This is the technique I'm most inconsistent about. My instinct is always to "finish" one topic before moving on. I've started structuring my study sessions explicitly: 25 min topic A → 25 min topic B → 25 min topic A (which is also a Pomodoro structure).

Connects to [[Deliberate Practice]] — the idea that optimal learning often requires fighting your instincts about what "feels productive."
