*Why We Sleep* (2017) — Matthew Walker

> [!warning] Caveat
> Walker's book has been criticised by some researchers for overstating findings and cherry-picking studies. The broad conclusions hold, but treat specific statistics with some scepticism. The core claim — sleep matters enormously for memory — is solid.

## Relevant to This Vault

The memory chapters are excellent. Main claims:

- Sleep is not passive — it's when memory consolidation happens (→ [[Sleep and Memory Consolidation]])
- SWS (slow-wave sleep) handles declarative memory transfer; REM handles procedural and emotional
- Sleep deprivation doesn't just impair performance — it impairs your ability to *form new memories*
- Naps produce measurable memory benefits

## Most Striking Finding

> Before sleeping, you've prepared the brain to learn. After sleeping, you've actually learned it.

The study Walker cites: students who slept between a learning session and a test retained significantly more than those who stayed awake, even when total time elapsed was the same.
