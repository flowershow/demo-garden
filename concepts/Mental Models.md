Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's partner at Berkshire Hathaway, is famous for advocating a "latticework of mental models" drawn from multiple disciplines. The idea: learning a few powerful frameworks from physics, biology, psychology, economics, etc. gives you tools that apply across domains.

> [!quote] Charlie Munger
> "You must know the big ideas in the big disciplines and use them routinely — all of them, not just a few."

## What Is a Mental Model?

A mental model is a simplified representation of how something works. Not reality itself, but a useful approximation that helps you reason and make decisions.

Some models are descriptive ("here's how this system behaves"). Others are normative ("here's how to make better decisions"). The best ones are both.

## A Short List of High-Value Models

**From physics:**
- **First principles thinking** — decompose a problem to its fundamental truths, reason up from there (Feynman, Musk)
- **Critical mass** — some systems tip dramatically once a threshold is crossed

**From biology:**
- **Evolution / selection** — systems that can vary and select for fitness improve over time
- **Feedback loops** — see [[How We Learn]] for how these apply to learning itself

**From psychology:**
- **Confirmation bias** — we seek information that confirms what we already believe
- **Illusion of explanatory depth** — we think we understand things better than we do (relevant to [[The Feynman Technique]])

**From economics:**
- **Opportunity cost** — every choice is a rejection of alternatives
- **Compounding** — small consistent improvements accumulate dramatically (applies to [[Spaced Repetition]])

**From systems thinking:**
- **Stocks and flows** — understanding what accumulates (stocks) vs. what changes them (flows)
- **Feedback loops** — reinforcing vs. balancing

> [!tip] Mental models for learning
> The most directly applicable models to this vault:
> - **Compounding** → [[Spaced Repetition]] creates compounding knowledge
> - **Feedback loops** → [[Deliberate Practice]] requires tight feedback loops
> - **First principles** → [[The Feynman Technique]] forces first-principles understanding

## Mental Models vs Facts

Facts are isolated data points. Mental models are the *structures* that connect facts and let you reason about novel situations.

Learning a fact: "Ebbinghaus found we forget 60% within an hour"
Learning a model: "Forgetting follows a predictable curve that can be exploited with spacing"

The second form lets you infer what will happen in new contexts. This is what [[How We Learn]] calls *transferable knowledge*.

## Building Your Latticework

The goal isn't to memorize a list of models but to understand them deeply enough to reach for the right one intuitively. This requires:

1. **Encountering them in context** — read widely across disciplines
2. **Using them actively** — try to apply a model to a current problem
3. **Linking them** — notice when two models point at the same phenomenon from different angles

*This vault itself is an attempt at a latticework for learning science.*
